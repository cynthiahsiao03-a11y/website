Concrete Toboggan Electronics System

Sensor integration, strain gauge protection, and Arduino-based Screamometer design

January 2025 - Present Cynthia Hsiao

As part of the UBC Concrete Toboggan electronics team, I worked on systems used to monitor structural performance, environmental conditions, and rider interaction during testing and competition. The electrical system collected real-time sensor data to support quantitative analysis of mechanical loading on the skis, temperature regulation of electronics, and motion tracking throughout the course.

This data helped support design validation, safety verification, and informed design improvements for future iterations of the toboggan. My work focused on both sensor integration for structural monitoring and an Arduino-based Screamometer used as an interactive visual display.

Screamometer

I designed and programmed a Screamometer using an Arduino, a microphone input, and programmable LEDs. The system detected sound input from the microphone and changed the LED lighting response based on the measured input level.

This required reading analog microphone signals, processing input thresholds in Arduino code, and mapping sound intensity to LED behavior. The goal was to create a responsive visual effect that reacted to audience or rider sound levels during demonstrations and events.

Sensor Integration and Strain Gauge Assembly

For the sensor integration system, I worked on protecting embedded wiring and strain gauge components from corrosion, concrete exposure, and mechanical damage. Because the sensors were integrated into the concrete skis, the electrical components needed to survive moisture, alkaline exposure from concrete, and heat generated during curing.

Two 350-ohm strain gauges were embedded in each ski on the central bamboo reinforcement, where bending stresses were expected to be highest. These strain gauges were used to measure strain experienced by the skis during operation and were connected in a Wheatstone bridge configuration with precision resistors to improve sensitivity and reduce noise.

I assisted with the assembly of the strain gauge system, including wiring, protection planning, and integration of the Wheatstone bridge circuit. To protect the strain gauges, resistors, and wiring during casting and operation, heat-shrink tubing and RTV silicone were used to encapsulate embedded electrical components.

This protection helped prevent damage from moisture ingress, alkaline exposure from concrete, and mechanical stresses during operation. The bridge output was then amplified through signal-conditioning circuitry before being read by the controller for data logging.

Electronics Enclosure and Environmental Protection

The broader electrical system also included a temperature sensor to monitor the electronics enclosure and a heater that automatically activated when a predefined temperature threshold was reached. This helped maintain reliable operation in cold conditions during testing and competition.

A GPS module was also used to record latitude and longitude throughout each run, allowing the toboggan’s speed to be calculated and enabling performance metrics to be correlated with track location, including acceleration and deceleration zones.

All non-embedded electronics were housed in a dedicated electrical enclosure constructed with a Lexan outer shell and a PETG internal mounting plate. Lexan was selected for its impact resistance, durability, and ability to withstand cold temperatures, while PETG was used for the internal mounting plate due to its rigidity and ease of fabrication.

The enclosure was designed to protect electronics from direct impact, vibration, snow exposure, and environmental damage while still allowing access for maintenance. The electrical box was mounted near the back right of the toboggan to reduce interference with mechanical subsystems while keeping the system protected.

6-DoF Robotic Hand for Prosthetics

Mechanical redesign and structural validation of a low-cost myoelectric prosthetic hand

September 2025 - April 2026 Cynthia Hsiao

As part of IGEN 330, I worked on a team developing a low-cost, 6-DoF myoelectric prosthetic hand based on the Talos robotic hand platform. Our goal was to adapt the system for prosthetic use while improving affordability, modularity, and intuitive control.

I contributed to the mechanical redesign of the wrist assembly to integrate an Electronic Quick Connect (EQC), enabling secure attachment to a socket while supporting reliable power and signal transfer between the hand and control system. The design needed to balance strength, manufacturability, serviceability, and anatomical proportions.

Structural performance was validated using Finite Element Analysis (FEA) under a 300 N palm load to ensure the wrist and EQC interface could withstand realistic pushing and weight-bearing conditions.

Electronic Quick Connect Integration

I led the mechanical integration of an Electronic Quick Connect into the prosthetic wrist. Because the wrist experiences high bending loads during grasping and pushing motions, I redesigned the wrist-frame interface to improve load transfer and reduce stress concentrations.

The updated design used interlocking geometry and increased bearing surface area to shift loads away from fasteners and improve structural robustness. This helped create a stronger connection between the wrist, palm, and frame while keeping the system modular and serviceable.

The redesign also prioritized minimizing added length from the EQC to preserve anatomical proportions, reducing unnecessary weight through strategic material removal, and maintaining manufacturability through additive manufacturing.

Prosthetic Demonstration Device Design

I contributed to the integration and assembly of the Talos-based 6-DoF prosthetic hand into a modular, wearable system that combined mechanical structure, embedded electronics, and control hardware.

The hand was mounted to a custom wrist and palm assembly that housed intermediate electronics and interfaced with industry-standard prosthetic components, supporting compatibility with conventional socket systems. The design emphasized modular subassemblies to simplify servicing, battery access, and electronics troubleshooting.

To support user testing, we developed a wearable demonstration jig that allowed non-amputee testers to safely evaluate control, weight distribution, and balance before full socket integration. Multiple concepts were evaluated using a decision matrix, and a parallel-hand configuration was selected to reduce bending loads and improve realistic weight perception.

Finite Element Analysis

To ensure the redesigned wrist and EQC interface could withstand real-world loading, I conducted structural verification using Finite Element Analysis. The primary validation case simulated a 300 N quasi-static palm load to represent pushing or weight-bearing through the hand.

The analysis compared baseline and updated wrist designs by evaluating maximum von Mises stress, peak displacement, reaction forces at mounting interfaces, and estimated factor of safety. These results guided design refinements and helped validate the durability of the final wrist assembly.
